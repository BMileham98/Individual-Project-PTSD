About PTSD

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) is a mental health condition arising from experiencing or witnessing a traumatic event. Though it is commonly associated with soldiers, this condition may affect anyone regardless of age or life experience. The aim of this site is to help raise awareness for PTSD and advocate for survivors, as well as help younger survivors understand their feelings as well as learn how to cope. We hope this leads to a more empathetic society where survivors feel less lonely and are able to build a happier future for themselves!

Understanding the five fear responses

The Fight Response

A natural reaction to perceived threats, we may become defensive or aggressive in an attempt to protect ourselves. This may cause us to lash out physically or verbally.

The Flight Response

Hormones signal for us to avoid or escape the perceived threat. This may involve physically escaping the current situation or avoiding any situation that may lead to confrontation.

The Freeze Response

Stress leads us to become immobile, which may be compared to stage fright. Our emotions might numb themselves to protect us from overwhelming situations.

The Flop Response

This may be seen as a more intense version of the freeze response, our bodies and minds temporarily shut down to prevent further harm. This may be compared to playing dead.

The Fawn Response

We may try to avoid conflict or further harm through appealing to the threat, also known as people-pleasing. This can become a bad habit of neglecting ourselves to keep the peace.

What ARE fear responses?

Fear responses are the body's way of protecting ourselves from threats, evolving from the need to survive in dangerous situations such as while hunting. These may seem counter-productive today, with certain responses such as Freeze, Flop and Fawn being less understood and instead leading to stigma. However, these responses are still vital in protecting us during traumatic situations, and as such it is important to raise awareness about them. With this, we can reduce shame and increase empathy, leading to a happier and healthier future for survivors.

Symptoms of PTSD

Flashbacks

The most common symptom of PTSD is re-living traumatic events as though they are happening in the moment. This may not be literal memories playing in your mind but sensations, such as sound, smell and touch. It can also manifest as disturbing memories, nightmares or a sense of dread, as well as physical symptoms such as nausea or shivering.

These feelings may be triggered by familiar places or situations, even subconscious signals that may lead to confusion and distress.

Depression and emotional struggles

Living with trauma may cause conflicting feelings, such as explosions of anger and sadness or numbness and a lack of interest or motivation. This may stifle positive feelings like joy and evolve into fixating on negative thoughts, leading to low self-esteem, guilt and shame. It can also lead to loneliness, becoming wary of those around you and feeling anxious as a result.

It is common for PTSD to occur alongside other mental health conditions such as major depressive disorder or generalised anxiety.

Hyper-vigilence

It is common for PTSD to result in heightened awareness, leading survivors to frequently be on the lookout for threats. Even in seemingly safe situations, there may be lingering dread leading to anxiety and a need to remain constantly alert. This may affect sleep and make survivors easily startled, impairing concentration and causing hypersensitivity.

This may lead to panic attacks, sudden bursts of anxiety which can trigger physical symptoms like shaking, sweating and having a quick heartbeat.

Avoidance

Survivors often have the urge to avoid people, places and situations that may remind them of traumatic events, which may impair everyday life. When this is not physically possible, other measures may be taken to avoid fixating on the unwanted feelings. This may manifest as overworking or engaging in risky behaviours to keep occupied or numb the feelings as they come. Because of this, PTSD is often comorbid with various forms of addiction.


There are additional measures that the brain may take to avoid further traumatising experiences, such as dissociation. Dissociation is an experience where your emotions and sensations are numbed. This may have effects such as the world around you feeling unreal or you may struggle to recognise feelings within yourself. In severe cases, memories of past trauma may be blocked out partially or completely.

Don't suffer alone!

If you or someone you know is struggling with PTSD, please reach out for help. There are many resources available, including hotlines, support groups, and mental health professionals who can provide assistance.

Examples of additional resources include Mind, PTSD UK, and the NHS. These organisations can provide more information regarding the science behind PTSD and also offer information on other mental health conditions that may be co-morbid with PTSD. Treatment can be found through the NHS, which offers a range of therapies such as Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) and Eye Movement Desensitisation and Reprocessing (EMDR). These therapies can help survivors process their trauma and develop coping strategies.

If you need urgent assistance, there are helplines available:

Mind: 0300 123 3393
Samaritans: 116 123
If you or someone else are in danger of harm, please call 999
